The Ghost Job Index:
Q2 2026

May 21, 2026 9 min read TopAIJobs.com

There are two AI job markets. One is public — job boards, LinkedIn postings, Indeed listings. The other is real — referrals, network hires, executive recruiters working retained searches behind closed doors. The gap between them has never been wider, and most candidates are competing in the wrong one.

This isn't a theory. As of this week, a "Head of Applied AI" posting in San Francisco has been live on LinkedIn for ten months. Not ten days — ten months. In the same search results, a "VP of AI Success" in San Jose has been sitting for three months, a "Director of ML/AI" in New York for two. These postings appear right alongside roles posted in the last 48 hours. LinkedIn doesn't distinguish between them. To a candidate scrolling the feed, they all look equally real.

They aren't.

The macro data confirms what individual postings suggest at scale. Bureau of Labor Statistics JOLTS data from mid-2025 showed 7.4 million open positions nationally against just 5.2 million hires — a gap of 2.2 million roles that never converted to someone starting a job. A 2026 Clarify Capital study found that among senior-level postings specifically, roughly one in five is a ghost. And a ResumeBuilder survey found that four in ten companies admitted to posting a fake listing in the past year — with 62% of those saying the purpose was to make current employees feel replaceable.

But nobody had built a practical tool for filtering AI leadership roles specifically. So we did.

The Core Insight

The public AI job market is largely performative. Published research suggests that 27–40% of job postings across all industries aren't real. AI leadership roles are structurally worse due to budget uncertainty, internal politics, and investor signaling. The candidates who get hired aren't the ones who submit the best application — they're the ones who never use the application box at all.

The Ghost Score: Five Indicators

Ghost jobs aren't binary. A posting doesn't have to be outright fake to waste your time — it can be a budget-hold role, an internal-hire-already-selected role, a talent-pipeline exercise, or a comp-benchmarking tool disguised as a search. What they all share is that no external candidate is likely to get hired through the public posting.

We built the Ghost Score from five indicators, each grounded in published research on hiring behavior and ghost job prevalence. Each indicator adds one point. The higher the score, the less likely the posting converts to a real hire.

Indicator What To Check Why It Matters
1. Posting Age
45+ days		 Check the "posted" date on LinkedIn. Over 45 days with no updates or re-engagement. Indeed's Hiring Lab data suggests that past 45 days, the probability a posting is a ghost increases by roughly 70%. We found AI leadership postings live for 2, 3, even 10 months.
2. Comp Spread
$150K+		 Look at the salary range. Is the spread wider than $150K? (e.g., "$180K–$350K") A wide range means the role isn't calibrated — no approved level, no specific budget. The company is exploring, not hiring. We found one VP of AI posting with a $120K spread ($196K–$316K).
3. Repost Pattern Search the company name + role title. Find identical or near-identical postings from 60, 90, 120 days ago? Reposting the same role with identical copy is pipeline management, not active hiring. It resets the "posted X days ago" counter — making old ghosts look fresh.
4. Careers Page Absent Go to the company's website. Find their careers page. Search for the role. When an AI leadership role lives on LinkedIn but not on the company's own site, either the role isn't real or it's being handled without full internal alignment. We verified that companies actually hiring — like BlackRock and Credit One Bank — post AI leadership roles to both.
5. Reporting Ambiguity Look up the hiring manager on LinkedIn. Does the reports-to role exist? Does the manager show signs of team-building? If the posting says "reports to the CTO" but the company's CTO shows no indication of building an AI function, the role may be speculative — approved in concept but not in practice.

Scoring: 0–1 = probably real. Apply with confidence. 2–3 = proceed with caution. Verify directly before investing significant time. 4–5 = almost certainly a ghost. Don't apply cold — find an inside contact or move on.

What We Found When We Tested It

We applied the Ghost Score to AI leadership postings (VP, Director, Head of AI, Chief AI Officer) currently live on LinkedIn, cross-referencing with Greenhouse boards, Lever boards, and company career pages. Here's what the five-indicator test revealed in practice:

Posting age is worse than you'd expect. In a single LinkedIn search for "Head of Artificial Intelligence," postings ranged from 1 day old to 10 months old — all appearing in the same results feed with no visual distinction. Roles posted two, three, and four months ago are not active searches. They are artifacts. Yet they collect applications every day from candidates who don't check the date.

The careers-page test is the fastest filter. It takes 30 seconds and it's remarkably predictive. When we checked whether AI leadership postings also appeared on the company's own careers page, a pattern emerged immediately: companies with funded, actively-hiring roles — like BlackRock's VP of Applied AI and Business Innovation (posted April 13, reporting to the Chief Strategic Advisor to Aladdin) and Credit One Bank's VP of Artificial Intelligence — had the role on their own careers site with full job descriptions, specific reporting lines, and clear requirements. Companies whose AI postings existed only on LinkedIn or third-party boards were far less likely to have those specific characteristics.

Comp spread separates real roles from fishing expeditions. Postings with a salary range wider than $150K are not from companies that have approved headcount, built a level spec, and calibrated with their compensation team. They're from companies that want to see what's out there. That's market research, not hiring. One current VP of AI posting we reviewed listed a range of $196K–$316K — a $120K spread that signals "we haven't decided what level this role is." Compare that with companies like BlackRock, where AI leadership roles don't even list a range publicly — because at that level, comp is negotiated individually against a specific, pre-approved band. The absence of a posted range at a known-premium employer is, paradoxically, a stronger signal of a real role than a range that tries to cover every possible seniority level.

Why AI Leadership Roles Are Structurally Ghost-Prone

The ghost job problem exists across the entire labor market — ResumeUp.AI's analysis found that 27.4% of all U.S. LinkedIn listings qualify as probable ghosts, with that figure rising above 30% in cities like Los Angeles. But AI leadership roles have structural characteristics that make them worse.

Budget uncertainty. AI initiatives are expensive and their ROI is genuinely hard to forecast. Companies approve the headcount, post the role, then freeze the budget when the board asks harder questions about unit economics. The posting stays live because nobody tells recruiting to take it down — and because a live "VP of AI" posting signals to investors and the market that the company is "serious about AI." The posting itself becomes the deliverable.

Internal politics. Many AI leadership hires are ultimately decided at the C-suite level, often through CEO network referrals or board introductions — not the application pool. The public posting exists to satisfy HR process requirements, to benchmark the pre-selected internal candidate's compensation expectations against market rates, or to create optionality in case the preferred candidate falls through. A ResumeBuilder survey found that 81% of recruiters acknowledged their employers post ghost jobs, and nearly a quarter said the purpose was to gauge how replaceable current employees are.

Role confusion. The AI function is still being defined at most companies. A "VP of AI" at one organization is a team of 40 reporting to the CTO, building production ML systems. At another, it's a solo hire reporting to the CFO who wants "an AI strategy." The more ambiguously a role is scoped, the more likely it is to stall, restructure, or evaporate. That VP AI Innovation posting with the $120K comp spread? Its description included everything from "define AI roadmaps" to "transform client workflows" to "establish AI governance frameworks." When a single role tries to be strategist, builder, and compliance officer simultaneously, it usually ends up being none of them — because the company hasn't done the internal work of deciding what it actually needs.

The Real Hiring System

Here's what the Ghost Score data points to: the public AI job market — the one made of LinkedIn postings and Indeed listings and Greenhouse boards — is increasingly a performance layer. It exists, things happen in it, but the consequential decisions are being made somewhere else.

The actual AI leadership hiring system works like this:

Retained executive search. The highest-impact AI leadership hires — CAIO, VP of AI at a Fortune 500, Head of ML at a growth-stage company with real product-market fit — are filled through retained search firms working confidential mandates. These roles never appear on LinkedIn. The search firm identifies candidates, approaches them directly, and manages a process that's invisible to the public market. If you're applying to a posted AI leadership role at a major company, you're already behind the candidates who were recruited into the same process weeks ago.

Network referrals. Below the retained-search tier, the most common path to an AI leadership hire is a referral from someone the hiring manager trusts. This isn't a secret — it's how executive hiring has always worked. But it means that the application box on a LinkedIn posting is the lowest-signal channel available. It's not that applying is wrong — it's that applying without a parallel referral path is almost always a waste of time at the leadership level.

Behavioral signals outperform job boards. The companies that are actually hiring show it in ways that are visible if you know where to look: new AI team members appearing on LinkedIn over the past 3–6 months (they're building, not just posting); the hiring manager engaging with AI content or posting about team growth; recent funding announcements with AI-specific allocation language; conference talks or blog posts from the AI team (a team that's shipping is a team that's growing). These behavioral signals are more reliable than any job posting because they can't be faked the way a listing can.

The application box is where signal goes to die. Ghost jobs don't survive direct human contact. When you reach out to the actual hiring manager or get a referral from someone inside the company, you get the truth in 30 seconds.

How to Use the Ghost Score

Run this before every AI leadership application. It takes two minutes and will save you hours.

Step 1: Check the posting date. Over 45 days? That's a flag. Over 90 days? You're looking at a ghost. Over 6 months? That's a 10-month "Head of Applied AI" situation — a posting that exists for reasons that have nothing to do with hiring you.

Step 2: Check the comp range. If it's wider than $150K, the role isn't calibrated. If there's no range listed, check who the employer is — at a known-premium employer, no range can actually be a positive signal (comp is negotiated at level). At an unknown company, no range is just missing information.

Step 3: Search for reposts. Google the company name plus the exact role title. If you find identical postings from months ago, the company is running a standing pipeline, not an active search.

Step 4: Check the company careers page. This is the single fastest filter. Go to the company's website, find their careers section, search for the role. If it's LinkedIn-only, proceed with skepticism. If it's on both, that's a real signal — the company ran the role through its internal approval process.

Step 5: Verify the reporting line. Look up the hiring manager. Does the reports-to role exist on LinkedIn? Is that person showing signs of building a team? If the role says "reports to the CTO" and the CTO's LinkedIn shows no AI-related activity, team hires, or content — the role may exist on paper but not in practice.

If the score is 3+, do not apply cold. Find someone inside the company — a connection, a former colleague, an investor — and ask two questions: Is this role funded? Is there a timeline? If you can't get a direct answer to both, move on. Your time is worth more than a hope-and-pray application into a system that was never designed to hire you.

The Ghost Job Index is our attempt to get there faster, with data instead of legislation.

In the meantime: stop competing in the performative market. The real AI job market is smaller than LinkedIn suggests, more relationship-driven than any job board can capture, and far more efficient than submitting applications into the void. The companies that are actually building AI teams are showing it in their behavior, not just their postings. Learn to read the signals.

Action Steps

This week: Pick the top 5 AI leadership roles you're considering. Run the Ghost Score on each. Drop anything that scores 3+.

This month: For every role that scores 0–1, find one inside contact before you apply. A referral doesn't just improve your odds — it tells you whether the role is real before you invest the time.

Ongoing: Build a watchlist of companies showing behavioral hiring signals — new AI team hires, team growth posts, recent funding — instead of relying on job boards. That's where the real market lives.
